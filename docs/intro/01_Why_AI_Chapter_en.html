Chapter 1 · Introduction

Work Transformation in the AI Era

Three and a half years since ChatGPT's debut, AI is no longer a tool you merely "try" — it has become a collaborator you "work with." This chapter explores why we need to learn AI now, the three types of people who use AI, and why systematic design (a harness) is essential when handing complex work over to AI. Ahead of the technical concepts covered in Chapter 2, this chapter establishes the "why" — the motivation.

Why AI, Now

In the three years since ChatGPT's debut in late 2022, AI has fundamentally changed the way we work. It has evolved beyond a simple search tool or autocomplete assistant into a collaborator that writes reports, generates code, analyzes data, and designs presentations. It is now a reality that even non-specialists can use AI to directly build apps, documents, and analytical deliverables.

Korea is no exception. AI adoption among large enterprises has surpassed 70%, and policy support is accelerating as well, with the government's AI Basic Act taking effect and the K-Digital Platform initiative moving forward. Companies that have adopted AI report average productivity gains of 15–30%. However, adoption among small and mid-sized enterprises still lingers around 30–40%, making the adoption gap a major challenge.

AI is not optional — it is a matter of survival. But adoption alone is not the goal; how you use it is what determines real competitiveness.

What matters is not the fact that "we're using AI," but "how" we're using it. Simply asking ChatGPT a question does not create competitive advantage. What's needed is the ability to design AI agent systems directly, integrate them into an organization's work processes, and verify the quality of the results. This is the core of what this workshop covers.

Three Stages of AI Use

People who work with AI can be grouped into three stages of proficiency. Most remain at Stage 1, and this workshop's goal is to give you a first taste of leaping from Stage 2 to Stage 3.

StageWhat They DoAnalogy
1 User Asks AI questions and receives answers. Focuses on refining prompts to get better responses. A diner ordering from a menu at a restaurant
2 Practitioner Integrates AI into work processes. Delegates repetitive tasks to AI and uses the output directly in their own work. Someone who follows a recipe and cooks it themselves
3 Designer Designs AI agent systems directly. Divides roles, sets validation criteria, and builds complex workflows. A chef who creates the recipe and designs the kitchen system itself

Stage 1 users throw out single requests like "translate this" or "find the bug in this code." Useful, but limited for complex work. Stage 2 practitioners redesign their work around AI, thinking "AI drafts this report while I focus on strategic judgment." Stage 3 designers go further, thinking "this report needs research, analysis, writing, and review, so let's split each step across specialized agents" — designing the agent system itself.

This workshop's goal is to give you a first taste of leaping from Stage 2 to Stage 3. Today, Day 1 offers Stage 2 practice using an existing variant to run an AI agent team, while Day 2 offers Stage 3 practice designing a new variant from scratch.

Why Harness Engineering Is Needed

As you use AI in your work, you naturally run into limitations: "errors pile up the more I ask for revisions," "result quality is inconsistent every time," "complex tasks can't be produced in one shot." This is what it's like to work with AI without systematic design (a harness).

✗ Without a Harness
  • Everything requested from a single AI
  • Errors accumulate with each revision
  • Context overload — earlier parts get forgotten as things grow longer
  • Result quality is inconsistent every time
  • Complex tasks are nearly impossible
✓ With a Harness
  • Roles are separated and assigned to specialized agents
  • Step-by-step verification catches errors early
  • Each agent focuses only on its own role
  • Validation criteria make results stable
  • Complex tasks become structurally achievable

Harness Engineering is a design discipline that gives AI roles, rules, and validation criteria. It upgrades a simple command prompt into a structured system of work instructions.

To put it in an analogy, an AI without a harness is like a new employee who's only been told, "just work freely!" They're motivated, but don't know where to start, can't recognize their own mistakes, and have unclear scope of responsibility. An AI with a harness is like a team with clear role separation, verification, and safeguards in place — "this role handles data research exclusively, this role handles document writing exclusively, review must always be performed by a different role, and edits are only made within a range that can be reverted via git."

The technical details of harness engineering (orchestrator-specialist-handoff contracts, orchestration patterns, and more) are covered in depth in Chapter 2. This chapter only establishes the motivation for "why a harness is needed."

Multi-Agent: The Principle of an Expert Team

Handing everything to a single AI creates three structural limitations: context overload (packing all information into one place causes earlier parts to fade), role conflict (reviewing your own writing means missing your own blind spots), and lack of parallelism (processing tasks sequentially even when they could run at the same time, wasting time). These same limitations show up in human organizations too.

The solution is simple. By the same principle that a company separates planners, developers, and QA, AI can also be split into role-based agents that collaborate. This is the multi-agent system.

Single AgentMulti-Agent Team
Analogy A single all-purpose new hire An expert team (planning · development · QA)
Context All information in one place → overload Separated by role → focus
Verification Reviews its own output → limited Author ≠ reviewer → independent perspective secured
Parallelism Sequential processing → linear time Independent tasks run concurrently → time saved
Complexity Suited to simple tasks Suited to complex workflows

This principle is not tied to any specific AI tool. Whether you use Claude Code, Claude App, Antigravity, Cursor, GitHub Copilot, or anything else, the concept of "splitting roles and separating verification" applies the same way. In this workshop, we'll use all four tools and experience firsthand how the same multi-agent concept is implemented differently across each.

Where This Applies

Multi-agent teams take many different forms depending on the business domain. A common pattern is the pipeline structure of "research → analysis → writing → review," with each step handled by a specialized agent. Here are a few examples from the areas where time savings are most pronounced in real-world work.

TaskTraditional TimeWith an AI Agent TeamReduction
Market research report 3 hours ~20 min ~90%↓
Competitor analysis Half a day ~30 min ~85%↓
Proposal draft writing Half a day ~1 hour ~75%↓
Presentation creation 2 hours ~10 min ~90%↓
Regular report writing 1 hour ~15 min ~75%↓
Code review & bug fixing 1–2 hours ~20 min ~80%↓

One caveat: time savings do not mean "AI replaces people." When AI produces a fast draft, people can invest the time saved into high-value activities AI can't do, such as strategic judgment, critical review, and client communication. If a draft is ready in 20 minutes, the remaining 2 hours and 40 minutes can go toward verifying accuracy, adding strategic insight, and tailoring the message for the audience.

When AI quickly delivers 80% of the work, people create 100% of the value in the remaining 20%. The core strategy of using AI

Klarna (the Swedish fintech company) offers a representative case. In the first month after deploying an AI assistant, AI handled 2.3 million customer conversations — equivalent to the workload of roughly 700 agents. But instead of laying off those 700 agents, the company reassigned them to complex support cases and customer relationship management. It's a structure where AI handles simple repetitive tasks while people handle complex judgment.
